The Nominal Group: MY SONESIS

A noun group (phrase or clause) is a group of words that is made up of a noun or pronoun and words to describe that noun or pronoun. It is a group of words that has a noun or a noun equivalent as its headword. Within a nominal group, the headword (h) can appear alone, it can be preceded by a modifier (m) and a qualifier (q) can also appear after it. Take note that the modifier and the qualifier are optional since, as we have said, the headword can appear alone.

Take note also that, a group can be defined as a word or a combination of words that has a head word. If a combination of words does not have a headword, it cannot be referred to as a group. A headword is, therefore, the obligatory element of a group. When a group contains a single word, it is referred to as a single group but when it consists of more than one word, it is known as a complex group.

Every lexical word in English has (there is) a group that corresponds to it.

Examples of Noun Phrases

Examples of simple noun phrases include:

  • The house is built on the mountain top.
  • My gray dog killed the rat that crept into the class.
  • The dedicated teacher was rewarded for his dedication.
  • The clever child made his parents proud.

The Structure of the Noun Group in English

There are four major types of Noun groups in English:

  • The Unmodified Head Type: This type has no modifier or qualifier. Examples: Girls are here, Bode is handsome.
  • The Pre-Modified Head Type: This type has a modifier but no qualifier. Examples: The girls are here, Good cars are expensive.
  • The Post-Modified Head Type: This type has a qualifier after the headword but no modifier before it. Examples: Lawyers of our time are liars, Something delicious is what I need.
  • The Pre- and Post-Modified Head Type: This type has both the modifier and the qualifier. Examples: The young man in my room has traveled, All the other students who refused to buy the study material will be penalized.

Function of the Noun Phrase

The noun phrase performs various functions in a sentence:

  • As the subject of a verb: "The faithful servant was rewarded by his master."
  • As the object of a verb: "He disclosed the fact of the case."
  • As subject complement: "Mr. Stone was appointed the new principal."
  • As object complement: "He sold his son into slavery."
  • As a noun in apposition: "Princess Taiwo Williams, the CEO of Sunshine Group of Schools, traveled to Canada last night."
  • As complement or object of a preposition: "A noun phrase could function as the object or complement of a preposition in a sentence."
  • As a participial or participle: "Look at the eating otter."

Noun Clauses and Finite Verbs

As Subject of a Verb:

The faithful servant was rewarded by his master.

His words were untrue.

The quick brown fox escaped by the front door.

As Infinitive Phrases (Subjects and Objects of a Verb):

To beat small children is strictly forbidden.

To absent oneself from duty is an offense.

My dream after school is to get married to a handsome and rich man.

His intention was to beat the stranger black and blue.

As Gerundive Phrases:

Fetching wood from the forest is not an easy task.

Leaving the girls behind was never intentional.

As Adjective Headwords of a Noun Phrase:

The rich are loved by all.

The poor are helped by the government.

The masses are left to suffer during fuel crises.

As Object of a Verb:

I bought my wife a brand new car.

He disclosed the fact of the case.

The teacher beats the students mercilessly.

As Subject Complement:

Mr. Stone was appointed the new principal.

My wife is a nursing sister.

I am sure of my success.

As Object Complement:

He sold his son into slavery.

He called his brother a thief.

As Noun in Apposition:

Princess Taiwo Williams, the CEO and founder of Sunshine Group of Schools, traveled to Canada last night.

The reason, for his coming, was not known.

The story, of his sentence, is still a mystery.

As Complement or Object of a Preposition:

The music teacher sits beside the piano every day.

The man was aware of his stupid action.

He runs across the road foolishly.

As Participial or Participle Phrases (Turning Verbs into Adjectives):

Look at the eating otter.

See the swimming otter.

The boy is holding a bag containing candies.

He, reading the book, cries.

More Examples and Illustrations:

Remember that a noun clause must contain a finite verb. Now a finite verb is a verb that agrees in a sentence with the subject in terms of number (singular/plural), persons (first, second, or third persons). It also shows tense (present or past tense).

Types of Phrases

Phrases in English can be classified into different types:

  • Noun Phrase: The noun phrase is a group of words that is made up of a noun or pronoun and words that describe that noun or pronoun. It performs the function of an ordinary noun in the sentence, and it has a noun as a headword. Examples:
    • The house on the hill is beautiful.
    • His dedication to work is commendable.
    • That talented singer performs on TV.
  • Verb Phrase: The verb phrase is a group of words that includes a main verb and all its auxiliary (helping) verbs. It can also include modifiers or complements. Examples:
    • She is singing beautifully.
    • They have been studying hard.
    • We will have finished the project by Friday.
  • Adjective Phrase: The adjective phrase is a group of words that functions as an adjective to describe or modify a noun. Examples:
    • The girl with blue eyes is my sister.
    • He is wearing a shirt made of silk.
    • This book is extremely interesting.
  • Adverbial Phrase: The adverbial phrase is a group of words that functions as an adverb to modify a verb, adjective, or another adverb. Examples:
    • She sings with passion.
    • They ran very quickly.
    • He writes quite well.
  • Prepositional Phrase: The prepositional phrase is a group of words that begins with a preposition and usually ends with a noun or pronoun. Examples:
    • The cat is on the roof.
    • We went to the park.
    • The keys are under the table.
